# A Brief History of AI

The story of artificial intelligence is a cycle of bold ambition, sobering setbacks, and renewed momentum. Knowing the arc helps explain why the field looks the way it does today — and why certain ideas keep returning.

## Origins (1940s–1950s)

The intellectual groundwork came from people like Alan Turing, whose 1950 paper proposed the "imitation game" (now the Turing Test) as a way to think about machine intelligence. The field got its name in 1956 at the Dartmouth Workshop, organized by John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and others, who believed that every aspect of intelligence could in principle be described precisely enough for a machine to simulate it. Early optimism ran high.

## Symbolic AI and expert systems (1960s–1980s)

The dominant early approach was **symbolic AI** (sometimes called "good old-fashioned AI," or GOFAI): intelligence as the manipulation of symbols and explicit logical rules. This produced **expert systems** — programs that encoded the knowledge of human specialists as large rule sets, used commercially for tasks like medical diagnosis and equipment configuration. Symbolic methods were transparent and could explain their reasoning, but they were brittle: every rule had to be written by hand, and the systems failed badly outside their narrow domains.

## The AI winters

Twice — roughly in the mid-1970s and again in the late 1980s — inflated expectations collided with hard limits, funding collapsed, and the field entered an **"AI winter."** These downturns are an important part of the story: they're why practitioners stay wary of hype, and why the field's later successes were treated with caution at first.

## The statistical turn (1990s–2000s)

The next wave shifted from hand-written rules to **learning from data**. Machine learning methods — decision trees, support vector machines, ensemble methods, and probabilistic models — proved more robust than rigid rule systems. Around this time, IBM's Deep Blue beat world chess champion Garry Kasparov (1997), and statistical methods quietly came to dominate areas like spam filtering and search.

## The deep learning revolution (2012)

The modern era arguably begins in 2012, when a deep neural network called **AlexNet** (from Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton) won the ImageNet image-recognition competition by a wide margin. The result demonstrated that deep neural networks, trained on large datasets with GPUs, could dramatically outperform prior methods. The trio of Geoffrey Hinton, Yann LeCun, and Yoshua Bengio — later awarded the Turing Award — had championed these ideas through the lean years.

## The Transformer and the LLM era (2017–present)

In 2017, a Google paper titled "Attention Is All You Need" introduced the **Transformer** architecture, which became the foundation for nearly all modern language models. It enabled models like BERT and the GPT series, scaling to ever-larger sizes with striking results. The public inflection point came in late 2022 with the release of ChatGPT, which brought generative AI to a mass audience and set off the current wave of frontier models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, and others.

## The recurring lesson

A theme runs through this history, captured in Rich Sutton's essay "The Bitter Lesson": general methods that scale with computation tend to win over approaches that lean on hand-built human knowledge. The pendulum has swung from rules to learning, from small to large, and from narrow to general — and that direction has held remarkably steady.
